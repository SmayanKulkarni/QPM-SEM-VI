Module 5: Prediction
1. Using prediction in estimating
When using prediction to estimate time series data, you typically follow a series of
steps to build a model that captures patterns and trends in historical observations
and can then be used to forecast future values. Here is a step-by-step guide:
1. Define the Problem:
 Clearly articulate the goal of your time series prediction. Are you forecasting
sales, stock prices, temperature, or some other variable? Define the time
horizon for your predictions (e.g., predicting values for the next week, month,
or year).
2. Data Collection and Exploration:
 Gather historical time series data relevant to your problem. Explore the data to
understand its patterns, seasonality, and any trends present. Visualizations,
such as time plots and autocorrelation plots, can be helpful for this step.
3. Data Preprocessing:
 Handle missing values, outliers, and any other data quality issues. If needed,
transform the data to make it stationary (constant mean and variance)
through techniques like differencing or log transformations.
4. Train-Test Split:
 Split the dataset into training and testing sets. The training set is used to build
the model, and the testing set is reserved for evaluating its performance on
unseen data.
5. Feature Engineering:
 Extract relevant features from the time series data. This can include lag values,
rolling averages, or other domain-specific features that may help the model
capture patterns.
6. Choose a Prediction Model:
 Select a suitable prediction model based on the characteristics of your data
and the problem at hand. This could be a traditional time series model like
ARIMA or SARIMA, a machine learning model like linear regression or random
forests, or a deep learning model like LSTM or GRU.
7. Model Training:
 Train your selected model on the training dataset. Adjust model
hyperparameters as needed. For machine learning and deep learning models,
this often involves an iterative process of training, evaluating on the validation
set, and adjusting hyperparameters.
8. Model Evaluation:
 Evaluate the model's performance on the testing set using appropriate
evaluation metrics such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error
(MSE), or Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE). Compare the model's predictions
to the actual values.

--- Page 2 ---

9. Fine-Tuning:
 If necessary, refine your model based on the evaluation results. This may
involve adjusting hyperparameters, adding features, or trying different
algorithms.
10. Prediction and Monitoring:
 Once you are satisfied with the model's performance on the testing set, use it
to make predictions on new, unseen data. Monitor the model's performance
over time and update it as needed.
11. Consideration of External Factors:
 Depending on the nature of your time series data, consider incorporating
external factors that may impact the variable you are predicting. This could
include economic indicators, holidays, or other relevant external events.
2. Forecasting for autoregressive processes / forecasting for ARMA in
time series analysis
Autoregressive processes, often denoted as AR(p), are an essential component of
time series analysis. These processes model a variable as a linear combination of its
past values, with "p" representing the order of the autoregressive model. Forecasting
for autoregressive processes involves predicting future values of the time series
based on its historical data.

Here are the general steps for forecasting in autoregressive processes:
1. Model Identification:
 Determine the order of the autoregressive process (p). This can be done through
methods like autocorrelation function (ACF) and partial autocorrelation function
(PACF) plots.
2. Estimation:
 Use historical data to estimate the parameters of the autoregressive model. Common
methods include the method of moments or maximum likelihood estimation.
3. Model Diagnostics:
 Check the residuals of the model. Diagnostic tests such as examining whether there
is significant evidence for non-zero correlations at lags using autocorrelation in
residuals can be used.

4. Forecasting:
 Once the model is identified, estimated, and validated, use it to make forecasts for
future time points.
5. Prediction Intervals:
 Quantify the uncertainty of the forecasts by calculating prediction intervals. These
intervals provide a range within which future observations are likely to fall.
6. Monitoring and Updating:
 Continuously monitor the model's performance and update it as more data becomes
available. This helps to adapt the model to changes in the underlying data patterns.
3. Forecasting for general time series using infinite past
Forecasting a time series using an "infinite past" refers to using all available historical
data to make predictions about future values. In practice, however, it's not possible
to use an infinite amount of past data, as we are always limited by the data available
to us. Therefore, we typically use a subset of the historical data for training and
validation.
Here's a general process for forecasting time series data:
1. Data Collection: Collect historical time series data. This could be anything from
stock prices and weather patterns to sales figures and website traffic.
2. Data Exploration and Preprocessing: Analyze the data to understand its
characteristics. This may involve handling missing values, dealing with outliers, and
transforming the data to make it suitable for modeling.
3. Train-Test Split: Split the data into training and testing sets. The training set is used
to train the forecasting model, and the testing set is used to evaluate its
performance.
4. Model Selection: Choose a forecasting model based on the nature of your data.
Common models include:
 Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA): Suitable for
univariate time series data with a clear trend and seasonality.
 Exponential Smoothing State Space Models (ETS): Useful for time series
data with trend and seasonality.
 Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Networks: A type of neural network that
can capture long-term dependencies in time series data.
 Prophet: Developed by Facebook, Prophet is designed for forecasting with
daily observations that display patterns on different time scales.

5. Model Training: Use the training set to train the selected model. Adjust model
parameters as needed to optimize performance.
6. Model Evaluation: Evaluate the model using the testing set. Common evaluation
metrics include Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Mean Squared Error (MSE), and Root
Mean Squared Error (RMSE).
7. Forecasting: Once the model is trained and evaluated, use it to make predictions on
future values of the time series.
8. Iterate and Refine: If the model performance is not satisfactory, iterate on the
process by adjusting parameters, trying different models, or incorporating additional
features.
4. One-step ahead predictors based on the finite past: Levinson -
Durbin algorithm
The Levinson-Durbin algorithm is a mathematical algorithm used in signal processing
and statistics, particularly in the context of linear prediction. It is named after Norman
Levinson and James Durbin, who independently developed the algorithm.
The Levinson-Durbin algorithm is primarily used for autoregressive (AR) modeling of
time series data. Autoregressive models are a class of models where the current
value of a variable is modeled as a linear combination of its past values. In the
context of linear prediction, this algorithm is used to find the coefficients of an
autoregressive model that minimizes the mean square error of prediction.
Here's a basic overview of the Levinson-Durbin algorithm:
1. Input:
 Time series data (finite past values).
 Order of the autoregressive model (the number of past values to consider).
2. Initialization:
 Start with the first coefficient of the autoregressive model set to the ratio of
the covariance of the first and second data points.
3. Iteration:
 At each step, compute a set of reflection that describe the relationship
between the current residual and the previous residuals.
 Update the autoregressive coefficients using the reflection coefficients.
 Repeat until all coefficients are computed.
4. Output:
 The final set of autoregressive coefficients.
The Levinson-Durbin algorithm is computationally efficient and provides a way to
recursively compute the autoregressive coefficients, making it suitable for real-time
applications.

--- Page 5 ---

Linear prediction using the Levinson-Durbin algorithm is often applied in fields such
as speech and audio processing, where modeling and predicting future values based
on past observations are crucial.
In summary, the Levinson-Durbin algorithm is a powerful tool for estimating
autoregressive parameters from time series data, making it useful for predicting
future values based on a finite past.
5. The Kalman filter
The Kalman filter is a mathematical algorithm used for estimating the state of a linear
dynamic system from a series of noisy measurements. It was developed by Rudolf E.
Kálmán in the 1960s and has found applications in various fields, including control
systems, signal processing, navigation, and time series analysis.
In the context of time series analysis, the Kalman filter is particularly useful for
tracking and predicting the evolution of a system over time, even when the
measurements are subject to noise. The basic idea behind the Kalman filter is to
iteratively update an estimate of the true state of the system based on the current
measurement and the system's dynamics.
Here's a simplified overview of how the Kalman filter works:
1. State Prediction:
 Predict the next state of the system based on the current state and the system
dynamics.
2. Error Prediction:
 Predict the error covariance associated with the state prediction.
3. Measurement Update:
 Obtain a measurement and calculate the Kalman gain, which determines how
much weight to give to the measurement compared to the predicted state.
 Update the state estimate based on the measurement and the Kalman gain.
4. Error Update:
 Update the error covariance based on the Kalman gain and the prediction
error.
5. Repeat:
 Repeat the process for each new measurement.
The Kalman filter assumes that the system dynamics and measurement errors are
linear and that the noise is Gaussian and has a constant covariance. While many real-
world systems are not strictly linear, the Kalman filter is often used as an effective
approximation.

--- Page 6 ---

In the context of time series analysis, the Kalman filter can be applied to various
problems, such as tracking the position of an object over time, predicting future
values of a time series, or even in financial modeling to estimate hidden variables.
It's important to note that there are variations of the Kalman filter, such as the
Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) and the Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF), which can
handle nonlinear systems to some extent. These variations are especially useful when
dealing with systems that cannot be accurately represented by linear models.

1. Time Series & Automated Forecasting — Overview
A time series is a sequence of data points collected at equally spaced time intervals, where order matters
and past values influence future ones. Automated forecasting replaces manual model selection,
parameter tuning, and stationarity testing with algorithmic pipelines.
Key advantage of automation: one reproducible pipeline can handle hundreds of series
simultaneously, reducing expert time from days to minutes.
1.1 Time Series Components
• Trend: Long-term increase or decrease (e.g., rising annual sales).
• Seasonality: Fixed-period repeating cycles — daily, weekly, yearly (e.g., holiday spikes).
• Cyclicity: Irregular long-period fluctuations tied to economic cycles.
• Noise: Random, unexplained residual variation.
Decomposition: Y(t) = Trend + Seasonality + Cyclicity + Noise
2. Auto-ARIMA
2.1 ARIMA — Classical Foundation
ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) is the standard statistical model for univariate time
series.
• AR (p): Uses past p observations — captures autocorrelation.
• I (d): Differencing applied d times to make series stationary.
• MA (q): Uses past q forecast errors to smooth predictions.
ARIMA(p, d, q) — AR order p · Differencing d · MA order q
Manual selection of (p, d, q) requires reading ACF/PACF plots, running ADF/KPSS stationarity tests, and
subjective expert judgment — slow and error-prone.

2.3 AIC & BIC — Model Selection Criteria
AIC = 2k − 2 ln(L̂) | BIC = k · ln(n) − 2 · ln(L̂)
• k = number of parameters; L̂ = maximised likelihood; n = sample size.
• Lower AIC/BIC = better model (penalises unnecessary complexity).
• BIC penalises more heavily on large datasets → prefers simpler models.
2.4 SARIMA — Seasonal Extension
Basic ARIMA cannot model seasonal data. SARIMA adds seasonal components:
SARIMA(p, d, q)(P, D, Q)_m where m = seasonal period
• P, D, Q: Seasonal AR, differencing, and MA orders.
• m: Period — 12 for monthly, 7 for weekly, 4 for quarterly, 24 for hourly.
• Example: SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1)₁ ₂ — monthly data with yearly seasonality.
When to use: 1–50 univariate series with classic linear trend patterns. Fastest inference among
all automated methods.

3.3 Key Features
• Changepoint Detection: Automatically identifies trend shifts using a sparsity prior. Configurable
via n_changepoints (default = 25) and changepoint_prior_scale.
• Multiple Seasonality: Yearly, weekly, daily — all handled simultaneously with Fourier series.

• Additive vs Multiplicative Seasonality: Additive (default) — amplitude is constant. Multiplicative
— amplitude grows with trend (use for exponential growth).
• Uncertainty Quantification: Built-in confidence intervals using Monte Carlo simulation.
3.4 Automating Prophet — Grid Search + Cross-Validation
Hyperparameter tuning for Prophet is automated using grid search over key parameters:
• changepoint_prior_scale: Controls trend flexibility (0.001 to 0.5).
• seasonality_prior_scale: Controls amplitude of seasonal terms.
• seasonality_mode: 'additive' or 'multiplicative'.
Walk-forward cross-validation is used for evaluation — standard k-fold is invalid for time series as it leaks
future data into training. Prophet's built-in cross_validation() and performance_metrics() functions
automate this, returning MAE, RMSE, and MAPE per forecast horizon.
When to use: Business data with clear seasonality, known holidays, and stakeholder-
interpretable outputs.
4. AutoML for Time Series Forecasting
4.1 Why Machine Learning for Forecasting?
Traditional statistical models (ARIMA, Prophet) assume linear relationships and specific data structures.
Machine learning overcomes these limitations:
• Captures non-linear interactions and complex feature relationships.
• Supports exogenous (external) variables natively — promotions, weather, economic indicators.
• Gradient Boosting models (LightGBM, XGBoost) consistently outperform ARIMA on many real-
world datasets.
• Handles millions of rows efficiently; supports ensembling for better accuracy.
• Limitation: ML does not natively understand time — temporal structure must be injected through
feature engineering.

• Grid Search: Exhaustive; covers all combinations but exponentially slow — O(nᵈ).
• Random Search: 2–4× faster, similar accuracy; no learning from past trials.
• Bayesian Optimisation: Learns from prior trials; most efficient for expensive models.

Real-world forecasting often involves massive numbers of series — Amazon forecasts demand for 500K+
SKUs, energy grids manage 1M+ smart meter series. Training one model per series (local approach)
fails at this scale:
• Compute Explosion: 500K ARIMA models = days of computation.
• Cold Start: New products/stores have no history — local models fail immediately.
• No Cross-Learning: Similar series (same category/region) share patterns, but local models
cannot learn this shared structure.
5.2 Local vs Global Models
• One model per series. Fully customised but does not scale beyond ~1,000 series.
Cannot handle cold start.Local Model:
• One model trained across ALL series simultaneously. Scales to millions; learns shared
seasonal/trend patterns; handles cold start via features. Requires rich feature
representation of series identity.Global Model:
5.3 The High-Cardinality Encoding Problem
To build a global model, each series must be identified to the model. Store/product IDs are high-
cardinality categorical variables with potentially millions of unique values. Standard encoding methods
fail:
• One-Hot Encoding: 1M categories → 1M columns — memory infeasible.
• Label Encoding: Implies false ordinal relationship (store_1 ≠ 'less than' store_2).
5.4 Feature Hashing (The Hashing Trick)
Feature hashing maps any string identifier to a fixed-size vector of K dimensions using a hash function:
Hash: Series ID → h(x) mod N → Fixed-Size Vector [0, ..., N] →
ML Model Input
• Output size K is fixed regardless of vocabulary size — no growing dictionary.Memory
Efficient:
• New categories (new stores/products) are handled at inference without retraining the
encoder.No Pre-training:
• Same input always maps to the same hash bucket — no randomness.Deterministic:
• Multiple IDs may map to the same bucket — usually a minor accuracy trade-off,
controlled by choosing K (e.g., 128, 256, 512).Collisions:
In Python, sklearn's FeatureHasher(n_features=256, input_type='string') implements this natively and
integrates directly with any ML pipeline.

7. Decision Guide & Key Takeaways
7.1 Choosing the Right Method
• 1–50 series, linear trend, classic patterns → Auto-ARIMA (SARIMA if seasonal).
• Business data with known holidays and strong seasonality → Prophet.
• Many exogenous features, non-linear relationships, moderate scale → AutoML
(LightGBM/XGBoost).
• 100K+ series, cold start needed, operational scale → AutoML + Feature Hashing.
• When in doubt: baseline with naive seasonal model, then Auto-ARIMA, then LightGBM AutoML.
• Always validate using walk-forward cross-validation — never random splits.

7.2 Key Takeaways for Exam
• Automated forecasting removes the need for manual ACF/PACF reading and (p,d,q) selection.
• Auto-ARIMA uses AIC/BIC to select the best ARIMA/SARIMA configuration algorithmically.
• Prophet decomposes time series into trend, seasonality, holidays, and noise using a Bayesian

never leak into training.

Manual ARIMA: Challenges & Limitations
• 🔬 Expert knowledge required — must interpret ACF/PACF plots correctly
• ⏱ Time-consuming — testing each (p,d,q) combination manually is slow
• 📉 Subjective — different analysts may choose different parameters
• 🔁 Not scalable — impractical for hundreds or thousands of time series
• 🔄 No automated retraining — must be manually updated when patterns change
• ⚠ Stationarity testing — requires manual ADF/KPSS interpretation
• 🧩 Seasonality ignored — basic ARIMA cannot handle seasonal patterns (needs SARIMA extension)
• 💸 Cost — intensive analyst time = high operational cost for large-scale forecasting

Automating Prophet: Grid Search + Cross Validation
• Define hyperparameter grid: changepoint_prior_scale × seasonality_prior_scale ×
seasonality_mode
• Use Prophet's built-in cross_validation() for walk-forward evaluation
• Compute performance metrics: performance_metrics() returns MAE, RMSE, MAPE per horizon
• Select best configuration based on average RMSE across all CV windows
• Parallelise with joblib.Parallel or multiprocessing for faster search
• Typical grid size: 3×3×2 = 18 configurations, each evaluated across multiple windows
• Libraries: prophet, itertools.product, pandas, joblib
• Result: fully automated Prophet model selection with zero manual tuning

Walk-Forward Validation for AutoML
• Standard k-fold cross-validation shuffles data — invalid for time series (future leaks into past)
• Walk-forward CV: expand training window forward in time, always predict future only
• Minimum initial training size: depends on data frequency and desired stability
• Step size: how many periods to advance each fold (1 = rolling, n = blocked)
• Gap period: add buffer between train and test to avoid leakage from autocorrelation
• Aggregate metrics: average MAE/RMSE across all folds — more reliable than single split
• TimeSeriesSplit (sklearn): implements walk-forward for any ML model
• Prophet: cross_validation(model, initial='365 days', period='30 days', horizon='90 days')

Why Feature Hashing for Multi-Series?
• Problem: Store/product IDs are high-cardinality categorical variables (millions of unique values)
• One-hot encoding: 1M categories → 1M columns — memory and compute infeasible
• Label encoding: ordinal structure misleads the model (store_1 ≠ 'less than' store_2)
• Feature hashing: maps any string ID to a fixed-size vector of K dimensions using hash functions
• K is user-defined (e.g. K=128, 256, 512) — controls trade-off between accuracy and memory
• Sklearn: FeatureHasher(n_features=256, input_type='string') — supports string inputs natively
• Enables one global LightGBM/XGBoost model for all series — trained once, infers for all
• Combined with lag/rolling features per series → powerful global forecasting architecture

Cross-Series Learning with Global Models
• A global model trained on ALL series simultaneously learns shared structure across the dataset
• Series in the same category share seasonal patterns — the global model learns these jointly
• Regularization prevents overfitting to high-variance individual series
• Practically: LightGBM trained on 10M rows (across 100K series) outperforms 100K local models
• Transfer: a new series with zero history gets predictions from global model via its hashed features
• Meta-learning: the model implicitly learns 'what kind of series has what kind of pattern'
• Critical requirement: the global model must receive series identity as a feature (via hashing)
• Result: 100–1000× faster training time vs local approach with competitive accuracy

Decision Guide: Matching Problem to Method
• 📊 1–50 series, classic patterns, linear trend → Auto-ARIMA (SARIMA if seasonal)
• 🎉 Business data with known holidays, clear seasonality, stakeholder-friendly → Prophet
• 🤖 Many exogenous features, non-linear relationships, medium series count → AutoML
(LightGBM/XGBoost)
• 🏭 100K+ series, cold-start needed, operational scale → AutoML + Feature Hashing
• 🔁 When in doubt: baseline with Auto-ARIMA, then try Prophet, compare with LightGBM AutoML
• ⚡ Latency requirements matter: ARIMA fastest inference, neural models slowest
• 📈 Always validate with walk-forward CV — never split randomly for time series
• 🔬 Start simple: naive seasonal baseline first, then add complexity only when justified

Key Takeaways
1 Automated forecasting removes the need for manual model selection — machines search the parameter space
faster and more consistently than experts
2 Auto-ARIMA excels for univariate statistical forecasting; Prophet excels for business data with seasonality and
holidays; choose based on your data characteristics
3 AutoML transforms time series into tabular problems through feature engineering — lag features, rolling statistics,
and date features are essential
4 Feature Hashing solves the cardinality problem for global models — enabling one model to forecast millions of
series at operational scale
5 Always validate with walk-forward cross-validation and compare against a naive seasonal baseline before declaring a
model 'good'
