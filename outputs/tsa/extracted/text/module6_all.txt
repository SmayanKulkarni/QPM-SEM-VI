1. The ARCH Model (AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroscedasticity)
Introduced by Engle (1982), the ARCH model was developed to capture a fundamental feature of
financial time series: volatility is not constant over time. Periods of high volatility tend to cluster together,
as do periods of calm. The ARCH model captures this by making the conditional variance depend on
past squared values of the series.
1.1 Definition — ARCH(q)
A time series {Xₜ } follows an ARCH(q) model if:
Xₜ = σₜ ϵₜ , where σₜ ² = α₀ + α₁Xₜ ₋₁² + α₂Xₜ ₋₂² + ... +
αqXₜ ₋q²
• ϵₜ is an i.i.d. sequence with mean 0 and variance 1 (typically standard normal or t-distributed).
• α₀ > 0 and αᵢ ≥ 0 for all i ≥ 1 — ensures variance is always positive.
• σₜ ² is the conditional variance of Xₜ given past information — it is measurable with respect to
the past.
1.2 Features of the ARCH Model
The ARCH model is characterised by several distinctive statistical features:
• Volatility Clustering: Large shocks (positive or negative) tend to be followed by more large
shocks. If |Xₜ ₋ ₁ | is large, σₜ ² increases, raising the probability of another large |Xₜ |.
• Leptokurtosis (Heavy Tails): The unconditional distribution of Xₜ has heavier tails than a
Gaussian — consistent with observed financial return distributions. Even if ϵₜ is normal, Xₜ is
not normal.
• Time-Varying Conditional Variance: Unlike linear models where variance is constant, ARCH
allows the variance to change with the information set at each time step.
• Uncorrelated but Dependent: Xₜ is serially uncorrelated (E[Xₜ Xₜ ₋ ₖ ] = 0 for k ≠ 0), yet the
series {Xₜ ²} exhibits significant autocorrelation — revealing non-linear dependence.
• Mean Zero: The conditional mean E[Xₜ | past] = 0, since E[ϵₜ ] = 0 and σₜ is measurable on
the past.
Exam Tip: ARCH captures the 'quiet follows quiet, turbulent follows turbulent' phenomenon
observed in stock markets. Identifying ARCH effects: test whether the squared residuals of a
linear model show autocorrelation (Ljung-Box test on Xₜ²).

under what conditions does a strictly stationary solution exist?

Theorem (Strict Stationarity of ARCH(1)): A strictly stationary solution to the ARCH(1) model
exists if and only if E[log(α₁ ϵₜ ²)] < 0.
This condition is the top Lyapunov exponent condition. For the simpler case where ϵₜ ~ N(0,1):
• Sufficient condition: α₁ < 1 ensures a weakly (covariance) stationary solution exists.
• When α₁ < 1: The unconditional variance exists and equals Var(Xₜ ) = α₀ / (1 − α₁ ).
• When α₁ ≥ 1: The variance explodes — shocks do not die out and the series is non-stationary.
• Strict stationarity: Can exist even when α₁ ≥ 1 (i.e., even without finite variance), provided
E[log(α₁ ϵₜ ²)] < 0.
Economically: if α₁ < 1, a large shock gradually fades and the market stabilises. If α₁ ≥ 1, each shock
amplifies the next, causing an explosive, non-stationary process.
2. The GARCH Model (Generalised ARCH)
Proposed by Bollerslev (1986), GARCH generalises ARCH by allowing the conditional variance to
depend on both past squared observations AND past conditional variances. This gives the model
'memory' — volatility from several periods ago continues to influence today's variance.
2.1 Definition — GARCH(p, q)
Xₜ = σₜ ϵₜ , σₜ ² = α₀ + Σᵢ αᵢ Xₜ ₋ᵢ² + Σⱼ βⱼ σₜ ₋ⱼ²
The most widely used specification in practice is GARCH(1,1):
σₜ ² = α₀ + α₁ Xₜ ₋₁² + β₁ σₜ ₋₁²
• α₀ > 0 — baseline (long-run) variance level.
• α₁ ≥ 0 — weight given to last period's squared shock (reaction to news).
• β₁ ≥ 0 — weight given to last period's conditional variance (persistence).
• ϵₜ — i.i.d. with mean 0, variance 1.
2.2 Interpretation of GARCH(1,1)
α₁ Xₜ ₋ ₁ ² (Today's Shock) β₁ σₜ ₋ ₁ ² (Volatility Memory)
○ Impact of yesterday's surprise ○ Persistence of past variance
○ Captures immediate reaction to ○ High β₁ → volatility lingers long
news ○ Models prolonged post-crisis
○ High α₁ → sharp, reactive volatility instability
After a financial crisis, markets remain unstable for days or weeks. GARCH(1,1) captures this 'slow decay'
by feeding past conditional variance back into the model. ARCH alone cannot do this efficiently without
very high lag orders.

2.3 Existence of a Stationary Solution for GARCH(1,1)
Theorem (Weak Stationarity): A weakly (covariance) stationary solution to GARCH(1,1)
exists if and only if: α₁ + β₁ < 1
• If α₁ + β₁ < 1: The unconditional variance is finite and equals σ² = α₀ / (1 − α₁ − β₁ ).
• If α₁ + β₁ = 1: The model is called Integrated GARCH (IGARCH) — variance shocks are
permanent (unit root in variance). The unconditional variance is infinite.
• If α₁ + β₁ > 1: Explosive variance — the process is non-stationary and variance grows without
bound.
• Strict stationarity: A strictly stationary solution exists under the weaker condition E[log(α₁ ϵₜ ²
+ β₁ )] < 0, even when α₁ + β₁ ≥ 1.
Practically: GARCH(1,1) with α₁ + β₁ close to (but below) 1 implies high persistence — shocks die out
slowly. This matches empirical findings in equity and forex markets where volatility is highly persistent.
Exam Tip: ARCH(1) stationarity → α₁ < 1. GARCH(1,1) stationarity → α₁ + β₁ < 1. GARCH
generalises ARCH: GARCH(1,0) = ARCH(1). Higher β₁ means longer volatility memory —
crucial for modelling financial crises.
3. Bilinear Models — Bilinear Autoregressive (BAR)
Bilinear models, introduced by Granger & Andersen (1978), are one of the simplest forms of nonlinear
time series. They extend the linear ARMA framework by adding interaction terms between past values
and past innovations (shocks). These interaction terms allow the effect of a shock to depend on the
current level of the series — something linear models cannot capture.
3.1 The Bilinear Autoregressive (BAR) Model
The general bilinear model BL(p, q, P, Q) is defined as:
Xₜ = Σ aᵢ Xₜ ₋ᵢ + Σ bₖₗ Xₜ ₋ₖ ϵₜ ₋ₗ + ϵₜ
The simplest and most studied case is the Bilinear Autoregressive model BAR(1,1) or BL(1,0,1,1):
Xₜ = a Xₜ ₋₁ + b Xₜ ₋₁ ϵₜ ₋₁ + ϵₜ
• a Xₜ ₋ ₁ : Standard linear autoregressive component.
• b Xₜ ₋ ₁ ϵₜ ₋ ₁ : Interaction (bilinear) term — the key nonlinear component.
• ϵₜ : i.i.d. noise with mean 0, variance σ².
3.2 The Interaction Term — Economic Intuition
The term b Xₜ ₋ ₁ ϵₜ ₋ ₁ captures multiplicative interaction between past state and past shock. This
means the impact of a shock on future values is not constant — it depends on the current level of the
series:

3.3 Properties of BAR Models
• Non-linear Structure: The conditional mean E[Xₜ | Xₜ ₋ ₁ , ϵₜ ₋ ₁ ] = aXₜ ₋ ₁ + bXₜ ₋ ₁ ϵₜ ₋ ₁
is linear in Xₜ ₋ ₁ but nonlinear in the joint pair (Xₜ ₋ ₁ , ϵₜ ₋ ₁ ).
• State-Dependent Variance: Var(Xₜ | past) = σ²(1 + b²Xₜ ₋ ₁ ²) — variance itself depends on
the level of the process. This creates heteroscedastic behaviour.
• Stationarity: Requires conditions on the spectral radius of associated matrices. For BAR(1,1), a
sufficient condition for stationarity involves E[log|a + bϵₜ |] < 0.
• Moment Conditions: Second-order stationarity requires a² + b²σ² < 1.
Exam Tip: The key distinguishing feature of bilinear models is the product term Xₜ₋ₖ · ϵₜ₋ₗ.
This is what makes them 'bilinear' — linear in Xₜ and linear in ϵₜ separately, but not jointly.
They are the simplest models that capture amplitude-dependent noise effects.
4. Stochastic Volatility (SV) Models
Stochastic Volatility models take a fundamentally different approach to time-varying variance compared
to ARCH/GARCH. Instead of making volatility a deterministic function of past data, SV models treat
volatility itself as an unobserved (latent) random process. This makes SV more flexible but also more
difficult to estimate.
4.1 Model Specification
Xₜ = σₜ ϵₜ , where log(σₜ ²) = hₜ
hₜ = μ + φ(hₜ ₋₁ − μ) + ηₜ
• hₜ = log(σₜ ²): The log-volatility follows an AR(1) process.
• μ: Long-run mean of log-volatility.
• φ: Persistence parameter (|φ| < 1 for stationarity).
• ηₜ : Innovation in the log-volatility process, i.i.d. N(0, ση²).
• ϵₜ : Return innovation, i.i.d. N(0,1), typically independent of ηₜ .

5. Nonlinear Autoregressive Models (NAR and NARX)
5.1 NAR — Nonlinear Autoregressive Model
The NAR model generalises the linear AR model by replacing the linear combination of past values with
an arbitrary nonlinear function f:
Xₜ = f(Xₜ ₋₁, Xₜ ₋₂, ..., Xₜ ₋p) + ϵₜ
• f(·): A nonlinear function — could be a neural network, polynomial, sigmoid, piecewise linear
(threshold), or kernel function.
• ϵₜ : i.i.d. noise with mean 0 and constant variance σ².
• p: The lag order (number of past values used).
NAR is the most general framework for nonlinear univariate time series. The choice of f determines the
specific model type:
• Polynomial NAR: f includes cross-products of lag terms — captures smooth nonlinearities.
• Threshold AR (TAR): f is piecewise linear, switching regimes based on a threshold variable.
• Neural Network AR (NNAR): f is a feedforward neural network — can approximate any
continuous function.
5.2 NARX — Nonlinear Autoregressive with Exogenous Inputs
NARX extends NAR by incorporating external (exogenous) input variables Uₜ alongside the lagged
outputs:
Xₜ = f(Xₜ ₋₁, ..., Xₜ ₋p, Uₜ ₋₁, ..., Uₜ ₋r) + ϵₜ
• Uₜ : External input variable (e.g., temperature, policy rate, rainfall, price).
• r: Lag order for the external input.
• f(·): Nonlinear function — same options as NAR (neural network, polynomial, etc.).

NARX is the nonlinear equivalent of ARX (AR with exogenous inputs) and ARMAX. It enables the model
to use both the history of the target series and relevant external drivers:
• Electricity Demand: Xₜ (demand) depends nonlinearly on past demand AND temperature
Uₜ ₋ ₁ . A hot day causes a disproportionate surge in AC usage.
• Macroeconomics: GDP depends nonlinearly on its own history AND monetary policy inputs.
• Epidemiology: Infection rate depends on past infections AND vaccination rates.
5.3 Properties and Stationarity
• Stationarity of NAR/NARX: Depends heavily on the specific form of f. For f Lipschitz
continuous with Lipschitz constant L < 1, geometric ergodicity (a strong stationarity concept) can
be established.
• Estimation: Parameters of f are estimated by Nonlinear Least Squares (NLS), maximum
likelihood, or neural network backpropagation, depending on the form of f.
• Advantage over linear AR: Captures threshold effects, regime changes, saturation effects, and
asymmetric responses — all common in real-world data.
• Model Selection: Lag orders p and r selected by information criteria (AIC/BIC). The form of f is
chosen based on domain knowledge or via cross-validation.
Exam Tip: NAR = nonlinear f of past outputs only. NARX = nonlinear f of past outputs + past
external inputs. The key advantage is that real-world relationships are rarely linear —
temperature's effect on energy demand is nonlinear; NARX captures this naturally.

• GARCH(1,1): Extends ARCH by including past conditional variance (β₁ σₜ ₋ ₁ ²). Captures long

Xₜ = f(Xₜ₋₁, Xₜ₋₂, …) + ϵₜ
Uses a nonlinear function f
to capture complex patterns


🌦 Weather Example:
Temperature rises are non-linear — a small increase when it's already 42°C causes disproportionate discomfort.


Past values affect future in a
non-proportional way
🔗  NARX  (With External Inputs)
Xₜ = f(Xₜ₋₁, Uₜ₋₁) + ϵₜ
Uₜ = external input variable
More realistic real-world modelling


⚡ Electricity Demand Example:
Demand depends on:
  • Past demand history
  • Temperature (external input)


Hot day → sudden AC surge explains NARX over plain NAR
