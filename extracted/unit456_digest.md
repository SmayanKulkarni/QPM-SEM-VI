N = Total number of assets in the portfolio

It is called naïve because:
It does not consider risk

It does not consider correlations between assets

Naive diversification is dividing an investment portfolio simply and by instinct rather than relying on complex mathematical models.
Optimized, or sophisticated, diversification, on the other hand, utilizes complex mathematical formulas to diversify a portfolio.
Sophisticated diversification seeks to maximize gains and minimize risks but isn't always effective.
Complex models often have unknown variables and estimation errors, making naive diversification a better option in some instances.

Despite its name, the 1/N rule is not "stupid." It is a highly effective strategy in specific contexts:

Large Universes: When choosing from a large pool of assets (e.g., 500 S&P 500 stocks), 1/N is a safe proxy for market-cap weighting without the complexity.
Uncertainty: When you have low confidence in your ability to forecast returns or correlations.
Target Date Funds: Many Target Date Funds utilize a version of this logic, gradually shifting weights as the target date approaches.

Although it updates regularly, rolling covariance may not react instantly to abrupt market shocks. The presence of older observations in the window slows adjustment. This can delay risk recognition during extreme events.

- Slide 10: Limitations of Rolling Covariance Windows | 4. Can be Unstable with Small Window
A small window uses fewer observations, making estimates more volatile. Minor changes in data can significantly affect covariance values. This instability may lead to unreliable portfolio decisions.

5. Equal Weight to All Observations
Rolling covariance assigns equal importance to all data points within the window. It does not differentiate between recent and older observations. This can reduce responsiveness compared to methods like EWMA.

Negative correlation (-1) → securities move in opposite directions
Zero correlation (0) → no relationship
Best diversification occurs when correlation is low or negative.

- Slide 3: In finance, shrinkage estimators combine:
The sample estimate (based on historical data)
A structured or target estimate (simpler and more stable)
to produce a more reliable estimate.
General formula:
Σshrink=λT+(1−λ)S   or  (λ × Target) + ((1-λ) × Sample)
Where:
S = Sample covariance matrix
T = Target matrix
λ= Shrinkage intensity (between 0 and 1)
When λ = 0, you trust the sample completely. When λ = 1, you trust only the target.

2️⃣ Better Risk Management
The strategy controls risk through the cushion mechanism. As the cushion decreases, investment in risky assets is reduced. This limits potential losses in adverse market conditions.
3️⃣ Participation in Market Growth
CPPI allows investors to benefit from rising markets. When the portfolio value increases, more funds are allocated to equities. This enhances return potential while maintaining safety.
- Slide 22: How CPPI Helps in Portfolio Management | 4️⃣ Dynamic Portfolio Adjustment
The portfolio is continuously rebalanced based on market movements. It increases risk exposure in favorable conditions and decreases it in downturns. This ensures efficient portfolio management.
5️⃣ Disciplined Investment Approach
CPPI follows a rule-based strategy rather than emotional decision-making. It automatically adjusts allocation without investor bias. This improves consistency in portfolio performance.
6️⃣ Protection of Gains
With features like drawdown constraints, CPPI helps lock in profits. As the portfolio reaches new highs, the floor can be adjusted upward. This prevents erosion of accumulated gains.

Transaction Risk • Arises from already agreed foreign currency contracts (imports/exports). • Exists between invoice date and settlement date. • Caused by exchange rate fluctuations during credit period. • Directly affects cash inflows and outflows. • Impacts profit margin on foreign trade transactions. • Short-term in nature (days to months). • Can be hedged using forwards, futures, options. Example An Indian exporter sells goods to a US buyer for $10,000, payable after 60 days. If USD falls from ₹83 to ₹80, the exporter receives less in INR, causing a loss due to transaction risk.

Translation Risk (Risk when financial statements of foreign subsidiaries are converted into home currency) Explanation • Occurs when a company has foreign subsidiaries. • Financial statements must be converted into reporting currency. • Affects balance sheet and income statement values. • Does not involve actual cash flow changes. • Arises due to exchange rate fluctuations at reporting date. • Impacts reported profits and net worth. • Important for multinational corporations (MNCs). Example An Indian company has a US subsidiary with net assets of $1 million. If USD depreciates from ₹83 to ₹80, the value of assets reduces in INR terms, causing a translation loss in books.
- Page 5: Economic Risk (Long-term impact of exchange rate changes on firm value and competitiveness) Explanation • Also called operating exposure. • Affects future cash flows and competitiveness. • Arises due to long-term currency movements. • Impacts pricing strategy, demand, and cost structure. • Difficult to measure and hedge fully. • Influences market value of the firm. • Requires strategic decisions like diversification or relocation. Example An Indian IT company earns most revenue in USD. If INR appreciates strongly (₹83 → ₹75), its earnings in INR reduce, making it less competitive globally and lowering firm value.

Furthermore, longer bullet strategies carry significant interest rate risk, and force investors to make a bet on a single point on the yield curve. - Slide 6: Barbell – Skip the Middle | While barbell strategies come in all shapes and sizes, most involve allocating capital to shorter term maturities, avoiding intermediate and investing in long. The longer end of the barbell provides duration exposure and offers more certainty around future cash flows, while the short end lowers overall portfolio volatility and provides more flexibility as bonds mature. Just like a bullet, barbell strategies involve making specific yield curve bets by excluding intermediate bonds, resulting in a portfolio that is overweight both short and long bonds.
- Slide 8: Ladder – Broad Exposure | In a laddered strategy, investors attempt to evenly distribute maturities across their investment horizon, providing exposure to short, intermediate and long bonds. When the shortest bond in the ladder (rung) matures, proceeds are reinvested into the longest end, keeping duration relatively stable. While the length of both barbells and ladders can be customized, a laddered strategy does not make specific yield curve bets over its investment horizon. Once the length of the ladder is established, broad exposure is the goal.

  - Basis | Active Management | Passive Management
  - 1. Objective | To outperform the market | To match market/benchmark returns

  - 9. Flexibility | Highly flexible | Limited flexibility
  - 10. Suitability | Suitable for aggressive investors | Suitable for conservative investors
